The Nicene Creed

A Timeline of Authority, Development, and Division

325 AD Council of Nicaea
+

Convened by Emperor Constantine to resolve the Arian controversy — whether Christ was truly divine and co-eternal with the Father, or a created being. Roughly 300 bishops attended.

The council produced the original Nicene Creed, using the non-biblical term homoousios ("of one substance") to close off Arius's reading of texts like John 14:28.

Focused almost entirely on the Father-Son relationship; language on the Holy Spirit was minimal ("We believe in the Holy Spirit" — full stop).

First Ecumenical Council
Seed Question If Scripture alone was sufficient and self-clarifying, why did the Church need an authoritative council and a non-biblical term to settle what "the Son" meant?
381 AD Council of Constantinople
+

Called to finish what Nicaea left unfinished. Expanded the creed's treatment of the Holy Spirit ("the Lord, the giver of life, who proceeds from the Father... who has spoken through the prophets") and added the closing article on the Church, baptism, and resurrection.

This expanded version — the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed — is what nearly all Christians recite today when they say "the Nicene Creed."

Second Ecumenical Council
Seed Question It took two councils, 56 years apart, to fully articulate the Trinity. Doctrine wasn't self-evident from an open Bible — it was worked out, ruled on, and settled by a body with binding authority.
431 AD Council of Ephesus
+

Primarily addressed the Nestorian controversy (Christ's two natures, and Mary as Theotokos, "God-bearer"). In the process, the council explicitly forbade any further alteration to the Nicene-Constantinopolitan text.

From this point on, the creed's wording was formally closed — which makes the next entry on this timeline significant.

Third Ecumenical Council
6th–11th c. AD The Filioque Clause
+

The Western Church began adding "and the Son" (filioque) to the line on the Holy Spirit — changing "who proceeds from the Father" to "who proceeds from the Father and the Son."

It appeared first in regional councils in Spain (6th century) and spread gradually through the West, formally adopted in Rome by the 11th century — without an ecumenical council's approval, and in apparent tension with Ephesus's 431 ruling.

The Eastern Church never accepted it, viewing it as both theologically flawed and procedurally illegitimate — a unilateral change to a fixed, conciliar text.

Contested Addition
Honest Caveat This is real ammunition for the "even the Church disputes its own authority" objection. Worth knowing cold rather than being caught off guard by it — the honest answer involves distinguishing the East's procedural objection from an outright rejection of the underlying theology, which Rome holds is compatible with, not contrary to, Nicene Trinitarianism.
⟶ tension builds over three centuries ⟶
1054 AD The Great Schism
+

Mutual excommunications between Rome and Constantinople formalized the East-West split. The filioque clause was one of several major factors, alongside disputes over papal authority, liturgical practice, and jurisdiction.

The Catholic and Eastern Orthodox Churches remain divided to this day, though both hold the pre-filioque Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed as authoritative — they simply recite different versions of one clause.
