The Nicene Creed

Full Text (381 AD, Niceno-Constantinopolitan) with Structural Notes

We believe in one God, the Father almighty, maker of heaven and earth, of all things visible and invisible.

We believe in one Lord Jesus Christ, the Only Begotten Son of God, born of the Father before all ages. God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten, not made, consubstantial with the Father; through him all things were made. For us men and for our salvation he came down from heaven, and by the Holy Spirit was incarnate of the Virgin Mary, and became man. For our sake he was crucified under Pontius Pilate, he suffered death and was buried, and rose again on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures. He ascended into heaven and is seated at the right hand of the Father. He will come again in glory to judge the living and the dead and his kingdom will have no end.

We believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life, who proceeds from the Father and the Son, who with the Father and the Son is adored and glorified, who has spoken through the prophets.

We believe in one, holy, catholic and apostolic Church. We confess one Baptism for the forgiveness of sins and we look forward to the resurrection of the dead and the life of the world to come. Amen.

Underlined text marks the contested filioque clause — see notes below.

Structure Three "We Believe" Sections
+

The creed's three-part structure maps directly onto the Trinity: Father (creation), Son (incarnation and redemption), Holy Spirit (sanctification). It's essentially a Trinitarian outline of salvation history, not a random list of propositions.

Key Term "Consubstantial with the Father"
+

This is homoousios — the actual battleground word from 325 AD. Arius rejected it because it asserted Christ shares the Father's very substance, not merely a similar or derived nature.

Why It Matters The word appears nowhere in Scripture. It was chosen precisely because existing biblical vocabulary was being read two incompatible ways — a case study for how authoritative interpretation, not just the text alone, settled the dispute.
Key Clause "Who Proceeds from the Father and the Son"
+

This is the filioque clause ("and the Son") — added by the Western Church centuries after 381, never accepted by the Eastern Orthodox Church.

Know This Cold The Council of Ephesus (431) forbade further changes to the creed's text. The filioque's addition — without an ecumenical council — is a real point of tension, and a major factor in the 1054 East-West Schism. Worth having a ready answer rather than being surprised by it in conversation.
Key Phrase "One, Holy, Catholic and Apostolic Church"
+

"Catholic" here means universal — but the phrase does more than affirm a broad, invisible communion of believers. It attributes specific, visible marks — unity, holiness, apostolicity — to a real, identifiable Church.

Useful for Calvary Chapel Conversations Dispensationalist ecclesiology tends to treat "the Church" as more invisible and individualized. This line in a creed both traditions recite is a good entry point for asking what "one... Church" was actually meant to describe in 381 — a scattered, invisible body, or a visible, identifiable one with marks you could point to.
