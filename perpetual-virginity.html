Perpetual Virginity

The "Brothers of Jesus" Objection

The Objection
The Texts That Raise the Question
+

"Is not this the carpenter's son? Is not his mother called Mary? And are not his brothers James and Joseph and Simon and Judas? And are not all his sisters with us?" — Matthew 13:55-56

This is the strongest text for the objection, and it should be taken seriously rather than argued away too quickly. On a plain, modern English reading, "brothers and sisters" sounds like exactly what it says.

Their Likely Opening "The Bible plainly says Jesus had brothers and sisters. Why does the Catholic Church deny what Scripture says outright?"
The Linguistic Case
What "Brother" (Adelphos) Actually Covered
+

The Gospels were written in Greek, but they record events, sayings, and family relationships from an Aramaic and Hebrew-speaking culture. Neither Aramaic nor Hebrew had a separate word for "cousin" or "kinsman" — the same word used for brother (Hebrew ach, Aramaic aha) covered a much wider range of family relationships, and this usage carried directly into the Greek of the New Testament.

The Old Testament shows this pattern clearly. Genesis 13:8 and 14:14 both call Lot "brother" to Abraham — but Genesis 11:27 identifies Lot as Abraham's nephew, son of his brother Haran. The word is doing real linguistic work as "kinsman," not a loose figure of speech.

Know This Cold The Septuagint (the Greek Old Testament the apostles used and quoted) translates Lot as Abraham's adelphos in Genesis 14:14 — the exact same Greek word used for Jesus's "brothers" in the Gospels — despite Lot clearly being his nephew. This is the single strongest piece of evidence: it shows adelphos already carrying this broader meaning in the very Greek text the apostles were steeped in.
Identifying "the Brothers"
Who James, Joseph, Simon, and Judas Actually Were
+

Comparing Gospel texts closely, the "brothers" named in Matthew 13:55 appear to be identified elsewhere as children of a different Mary — not the mother of Jesus.

ReferenceWhat It Says
Matthew 27:56Names "Mary the mother of James and Joseph" standing at the crucifixion — a different Mary from Jesus's mother, who is named separately nearby (John 19:25).
John 19:25Identifies this second Mary as "the wife of Clopas," present alongside Jesus's mother — two distinct women both named Mary.
Mark 6:3Lists the same four "brothers" — James, Joses, Judas, Simon — matching the sons of this other Mary.

If this identification holds, James and Joseph — two of the four "brothers" named in Matthew 13:55 — are the sons of a different Mary altogether, making them cousins or other close kin of Jesus, not siblings.

Circumstantial Evidence
The Cross, and the Silence Elsewhere
+

"When Jesus saw his mother, and the disciple whom he loved standing nearby, he said to his mother, 'Woman, behold, your son!' Then he said to the disciple, 'Behold, your mother!' And from that hour the disciple took her to his own home." — John 19:26-27

This is a striking detail if Jesus had several living full brothers. In Jewish culture, care of a widowed mother fell to her sons as a matter of course — entrusting Mary to John, an unrelated disciple, is difficult to explain if she had other biological sons who could have taken her in.

Seed Question If Mary had other biological sons present at or near the crucifixion, why does Jesus hand her over to John — someone outside the family — rather than to his own brothers?
The Historical Testimony
How the Early Church Understood This
+

Mary's perpetual virginity was near-universally affirmed in the early Church, including by figures not otherwise known for Marian devotion. Notably, all three major Protestant Reformers held it:

FigurePosition
Martin LutherAffirmed Mary's perpetual virginity throughout his life and preaching.
John CalvinDid not treat the "brothers" as full biological siblings of Jesus; avoided firm statements against the traditional view.
Ulrich ZwingliExplicitly affirmed the perpetual virginity as part of Mary's honor.
Useful Framing This wasn't originally a Catholic-vs-Protestant dividing line at all — it only became contested much later, largely in American evangelicalism. Pointing out that the Reformers themselves held this view often lands better than any single Bible verse, since it reframes the question from "Catholic tradition vs. Scripture" to "a belief the Reformation itself never actually rejected."
