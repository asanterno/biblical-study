Reference Summary

Jesus and the Future

Understanding What He Taught about the End Times
Andreas J. Köstenberger · Alexander E. Stewart · Apollo Makara  ·  Weaver Book Company, 2017

A study of everything Jesus said about the future across the four Gospels — centered on the Olivet Discourse, but covering every eschatological saying. Its core argument: Jesus spoke prophetically about two distinct horizons, and most confusion in end-times debates comes from collapsing them into one.

Central Thesis

Two Horizons, Not One

The authors argue Jesus functioned as a genuine prophet, and like the Old Testament prophets before him, his predictions operated on two timescales at once:

Near Future

Persecution of his followers and judgment on "this generation" — culminating in the destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple by Rome under Titus, fulfilled in AD 70.

Distant Future

After an undefined interval, the Son of Man returning in power, the final resurrection, and final judgment with eternal reward and punishment.

The challenge — and the book's main project — is correctly sorting which sayings belong to which horizon, since Jesus often moves between them within a single discourse without flagging the transition explicitly.

Methodology

Against the Puzzle-Piece Approach

The authors' sharpest critique is aimed at a common method in popular end-times teaching: treating Scripture as a collection of interchangeable fragments that can be pulled from anywhere and reassembled to decode current events.

In Their Words (paraphrased)

A sentence lifted from Daniel, joined to a phrase from Revelation, used to explain a saying of Jesus — then mapped onto present-day headlines or astronomical events. This treats the Bible like a puzzle to be solved rather than a set of books each written in, and addressed to, a real historical situation. Each book must first be read in its own context before any synthesis across the canon is attempted.

Their corrective: read Mark 13 as Mark's Gospel first — written for a specific audience facing a specific crisis — before asking how it relates to Daniel, or to Revelation, or to current geopolitics.

The Olivet Discourse

"This Generation" and the Temple

The first half of the book — described by reviewers as the strongest section — works through Matthew 24, Mark 13, and Luke 21 in detail. Key conclusions:

  • Jesus' warning that "this generation will not pass away" refers primarily to the generation alive in his own day, pointing to the Temple's literal destruction in AD 70 — not a symbolic "generation" detached by millennia from his own audience.
  • Much of the discourse's vivid, cosmic-sounding language (darkened sun, falling stars) follows Old Testament prophetic convention for describing the fall of nations and cities — language also used of Babylon and Edom — and isn't necessarily a literal description of the physical end of the cosmos.
  • The discourse does shift, without clear seams, into genuinely distant-future material concerning the Second Coming itself — so both preterist ("it's all AD 70") and purely futurist ("it's all the end of history") readings oversimplify the text.
John's Gospel

Realized Eschatology

The second half surveys eschatological material outside the Synoptics, with a distinct section on John, which the authors treat as theologically different in emphasis:

Already

John stresses that believers possess eternal life now — in the present, not only as a future hope. Scholars label this John's "realized eschatology."

Not Yet

John doesn't deny the future dimension — Jesus still promises to return and prepare a place — but the emphasis tilts toward present fulfillment.

The raising of Lazarus (John 11) is read as a deliberate dramatization of this tension: Martha expects a future resurrection "on the last day," and Jesus shows her that he brings life right now, in the present moment.

Application

Relevance to the Temple / Covenant Debate

Two points from this book bear directly on the conversation with Steve about a future literal temple:

  • Methodology: The authors' warning against "puzzle-piece" prophecy reading applies almost exactly to the move of stitching Ezekiel 40–48, Zechariah 12–13, and Revelation into one continuous future timeline without first asking what each book meant in its own historical setting.
  • Already/Not Yet in John: The book's reading of John's "realized eschatology" supports treating John 2:19-21 (Jesus as the temple, already) as a deliberate present-tense theological claim — not a placeholder awaiting a literal future building. This is consistent with, not contrary to, your case from Hebrews 8:13 and Revelation 21:22.

Note: this book is historic-premillennial in orientation, not Catholic or covenant-theology in its conclusions — it's a useful hermeneutical ally on method, not a source that argues your land/temple-fulfillment case directly.
