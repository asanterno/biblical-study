Two Priesthoods, One Question:
By Blood, or By Oath?

Aaron's line traces a genealogy. Melchizedek's line traces none — and that silence is the whole argument.

Genesis 14 · Psalm 110 · Hebrews 7

Establishing the Levitical Line

Why lineage was not a formality but the entire legal basis of the office.

Exodus 28:1
"Then bring near to you Aaron your brother, and his sons with him, from among the people of Israel, to serve me as priests."
The office is created by direct address to one man and his sons — a hereditary grant, not a role open to application.
Numbers 3:10
"And you shall appoint Aaron and his sons, and they shall guard their priesthood. But the stranger who comes near shall be put to death."
The penalty clause shows how absolute the restriction was — this was never meant to be a permeable category.
Ezra 2:62
"These sought their registration among those enrolled in the genealogies, but they were not found there, so they were excluded from the priesthood as unclean."
Centuries later, after the exile, men who believed themselves priests were disqualified for a documentation gap — proof the requirement was taken with total seriousness, not treated as symbolic.

Establishing the Melchizedekian Line

A priesthood introduced with no ancestry, no successor, and no tribe — centuries before Levi existed.

Genesis 14:18-20
"And Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine. He was priest of God Most High... And Abram gave him a tenth of everything."
Abraham — the ancestor Levi himself descends from — submits to Melchizedek's priesthood by paying tithe. Hebrews will later argue this makes Melchizedek senior to a priesthood that doesn't exist yet.
Psalm 110:4
"The LORD has sworn and will not change his mind, 'You are a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek.'"
A messianic psalm applies Melchizedek's order — not Aaron's — to the coming king. The priesthood the Messiah holds was named a thousand years before Christ, and it was never the Levitical one.
Hebrews 7:3
"He is without father or mother or genealogy, having neither beginning of days nor end of life, but resembling the Son of God he continues a priest forever."
An argument from the text's silence: Genesis simply never records his lineage, his birth, or his death — unheard of for a priest in a culture obsessed with documented ancestry. Hebrews reads that gap as deliberate, a pattern built into the text pointing toward the Son.

The Argument of Hebrews 7

Not abolition of priesthood — replacement of its basis.

Hebrews 7:14 states it plainly: "Our Lord was descended from Judah, and in connection with that tribe Moses said nothing about priests." By the Levitical rule alone, wrong tribe ends the discussion — no exception clause exists in the Torah for a Judean priest.

This is why the argument can't simply graft Jesus onto the existing system. Hebrews has to establish an entirely different basis for priesthood before Jesus can hold the office at all.

Hebrews 7:9-10 makes an unusual move: "One might even say that Levi himself, who receives tithes, paid tithes through Abraham, for he was still in the loins of his ancestor when Melchizedek met him."

The logic: Levi didn't exist yet at Genesis 14, but he existed in seed form within Abraham. So when Abraham tithed to Melchizedek, in a real sense the entire future Levitical order — priests included — submitted to Melchizedek before that order was even instituted. Seniority, not mere difference, is the claim.

Hebrews 7:16 draws the contrast directly: Levitical priests were made "on the basis of a legal requirement concerning bodily descent," but Christ was made priest "by the power of an indestructible life."

One priesthood runs on inheritance law. The other runs on resurrection. That's why Hebrews 7:23-24 can say Levitical priests "were prevented by death from continuing in office," while Christ "holds his priesthood permanently, because he continues forever."

The letter is not claiming priesthood itself was a mistake or is now obsolete as a category — it's arguing a specific priesthood (Levi's) has been superseded by an older, higher-ranking one (Melchizedek's) that was always destined to receive it back.

This matters for the Catholic reading of ongoing sacramental priesthood: Hebrews dismantles the Levitical basis for priesthood, not the concept of a priesthood mediating covenant to God's people.

Seed Questions

For conversations where you're not trying to win, just trying to open the text together.

If priesthood in Israel required documented descent strict enough to disqualify men in Ezra's day — how does Jesus, from Judah, qualify to be a priest at all?
Why does Genesis go out of its way to record Melchizedek with no father, no mother, and no death — when every other figure in Genesis gets a genealogy?
If Psalm 110 says the Messiah's priesthood is "after the order of Melchizedek" — not Aaron's — what does that imply about which priesthood was always meant to be permanent?
Does Hebrews 7 read like it's abolishing priesthood altogether, or replacing one priesthood's basis with an older, higher one?
