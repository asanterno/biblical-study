✦ The Church & Its Wounds

Honest Formation — Scandals & Responses
"The Church is holy, though she has sinners in her care." — St. Augustine

Every scandal listed here is real. The Church does not ask you to look away from them. Honest faith requires honest history.

Think of the Church as a hospital. Its purpose is to heal the sick — which means it is full of sick people, including some of the doctors. The hospital's failures do not disprove medicine. They demand accountability.
The Wheat and Tares
Jesus himself predicted that the Church would contain both saints and sinners living side by side until the final judgment. This is not an excuse — it is a warning that applies to every generation.
"The kingdom of heaven may be compared to a man who sowed good seed in his field; but while his men were sleeping, his enemy came and sowed weeds among the wheat."Matthew 13:24–25
Institution vs. Teaching
A scandal committed by a pope or priest does not invalidate the doctrines they were supposed to uphold — any more than a corrupt judge invalidates the law. The Church teaches that her doctrine is protected from error; her members are not protected from sin.
Reform Has Always Come From Within
The most powerful voices demanding reform in Church history have come from inside: St. Catherine of Siena told a pope to his face that he was acting like a coward. Francis of Assisi rebuilt the Church through radical poverty. The Council of Trent reformed abuses the Reformation was exposing.
"Repentance is not weakness. It is the Church being the Church."
No Institution Is Clean
Every institution built by humans — Protestant denominations, governments, universities, charities — has its own history of abuse, cover-up, and corruption. The question honest faith must ask is not "Is this institution perfect?" but "Are its truth claims true?"
The Pattern of Apology & Reform
Pope John Paul II's Great Jubilee in 2000 included the most comprehensive public confession of institutional sin in the history of any organization — asking forgiveness for the Inquisition, the Crusades, treatment of women, and more. It set a model for ongoing accountability.
"We forgive and we ask forgiveness."Pope John Paul II, Day of Pardon, March 12, 2000

These are the objections you are most likely to hear from Calvary Chapel contacts or other Protestants. Here is the honest Catholic response.

"How can you join a Church with that history?"
The question assumes the alternative is a church without scandal — but there is no such thing. Every denomination has its own history of abuse and failure. The question is not whether an institution has sinned, but whether its foundations are true. The Church's failures are arguments against clericalism and pride, not against the Eucharist or apostolic succession.
"The Inquisition proves the Church is evil."
The methods of the Inquisition were morally wrong — the Church has formally acknowledged this. But the historical death toll was far lower than popular mythology claims (scholars estimate ~3,000–5,000 over 350 years for the Spanish Inquisition). Comparison: the Thirty Years' War, fueled by Protestant-Catholic conflict on both sides, killed millions. Institutional evil exists across all traditions. The Church's error was real; so was its eventual reckoning.
"The abuse crisis proves the Church isn't led by the Holy Spirit."
The Holy Spirit's guidance does not make members sinless — it keeps the Church from formally teaching error and ensures she endures. The abuse crisis was a catastrophic institutional failure of individual bishops, not a failure of doctrine. The Holy Spirit has also raised up survivors, journalists, and reformers who exposed it. Reform and accountability are ongoing — imperfect but real.
"Selling indulgences shows the Church invented doctrines for money."
The abuse of indulgences was condemned — by the Church itself — at the Council of Trent. The doctrine of indulgences (rooted in Scripture and early Christian practice) is not the same thing as Tetzel's fundraising scheme. A corrupt application of a doctrine does not disprove the doctrine. Luther himself, initially, only wanted reform of the abuse — not rejection of the doctrine.
"The Vatican's financial scandals show it's just a worldly power."
True — and reformers inside the Church have said so for centuries. Pope Francis has pushed more financial transparency and accountability than most of his predecessors. The Vatican's worldly entanglements are a recurring temptation the Church constantly has to fight. Jesus warned about this too: "You cannot serve God and money" (Matthew 6:24). The answer is ongoing reform, not abandonment.

The Church does not ask for blind trust — she asks for honest faith.
Honesty about her wounds is part of what makes her credible.
